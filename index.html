About

Welcome to my Learning Digital Archaeology website! As an anthropology student primarily interested in cultural anthropology, digital archaeology is somewhat of a foreign topic. I will post assignments, critical reflections, and other insights regarding anthropology in the ‘digital age.’

I will also share the trials, tribulations, and triumphs I encountered while learning the various methods and applications relating to digital archaeology. Things will be broken, tears may be shed, laughs are inevitable, the journey begins here.

Background

A little about me. My name is Suzanne, or Sue. I am currently in the final semester of my undergraduate degree (hurray!). I play hockey and have two dogs. I am the co-chair of the anthropology student union at my university. I always wanted to be a forensic anthropologist-thank you Bones, however inaccurate-until a professor explained how few forensic anthropologists there were in Canada, (she was the consulting forensic anthropologist for the Vancouver Island). Needless to say, my hopes were slightly dashed. Until, I took a seminar in culture anthropology and ‘found my calling’ or some such cliché phrase. I love museums. I am fascinated by people and ‘culture.’ My main focus is community-based and ethnographic research (aka cultural anthropology). Last semester, I completed the honours program, writing my essay on the perceptions of repatriation in anthropology literature (with what I’ve learned so far, I wish I took this class before/during the honours program). I took this class (Digital Archaeology: Virtual Heritage & Practice in the 21st Century) for a few reasons:
I needed another method and theory class to graduate.
It looked like a class that had genuine applicable job skills (somewhat of a rarity).
I am interested in digital archives and digital exhibits and the ethics surrounding them.
My digital skills are(were?) lacking, to say the least.

Portfolio 1: Critical Review

Critical Review of Website

Why did you design the website the way you did?
As someone who never built a website before, I wanted my website to be as simple as possible. When it comes to social media, excluding some Facebook proficiency, I am most familiar with food blogs. As such, when it came to designing my website, I found a one-page website template that met my requirements, needed little(ish) alterations, and had a colour scheme I enjoyed. My idea was to set up the website similar to a blog, where I could talk about how each project was working out. I like the informality and personal approach to blogs. How it is one person’s experiences and opinions. In food blogs the person walks you through the meal and how it turns out, why they wanted to cook and share with their audience. I will attempt to do the same, in the context of ‘learning’ digital archaeology.
What went wrong?
Though there were no catastrophic failures with my website (so far), a few things that went wrong include the photos chosen to replace the template ones were not entirely compatible. I forgot to commit changes before closing my browser once or twice and had to redo many things. I never did figured out how to scale down the size of my images. Overall, my website is very simple with minimal (no) bells or whistles.
How would you address these issues in future?
Spending more time learning to code and reviewing tutorials would help to address some of the problems I was having. Perhaps more trips to Office Hours would have also helped. More time learning code would have also provided me further abilities to add more ‘bells and whistles’. Over time I would like to change the photos selected for the projects section to something more applicable. However, I need to know what those project will be first.
What do you think is successful about the project? Why?
I managed to actually create a website. The links to ‘projects’ and social media work. I consider that a success. For someone who had no previous knowledge or skills of coding or technology in general, I am fairly pleased with the outcome of my website thus far. It can only get better from here!

Portfolio 2: Critical Reviews

Voyant

This platform is incredibly useful for analysing and interpreting large amounts of text data. I wish I had known about this software before; it could have helped tremendously for previous assignments. Voyant is fairly easy to use and the visualizations are easy understand. All you need to do is enter or upload text documents and the program gives you a summary of the text and creates word frequency lists, frequency distribution plots, word clouds and other visualization tools based on the data entered. This software is applicable to anthropology and archaeology because it allows you to analysis digital texts for word frequencies, correlations and associations between words. To try it out, I first uploaded Dr. Seuss’s ‘Green Eggs and Ham,’ there were many correlations between many words. To get a better idea of how Voyant worked, I uploaded my honours essay. My document had 9,959 words, 1,992 unique word forms, and the most frequent words were repatriation (177); article/s (112) American (75); and cultural (67). The visualization tools I preferred were the TextualArc and TermsBerry. I found them to present the data and correlations between words very effectively and visually pleasing. You can also export a ULR of a visualization tool or embeddable a tool to another platform. With some of the visualization tools, the amount of words in your document does affect their appearance. Some tools are better than others, and I have not completely figured out how to read all the various graphs, however, this platform is incredibly useful, I will use it again and would recommend this to anyone who wanted to analysis or visualize digital texts.
